Digital Financial Services · Financial Inclusion · Township Commerce

Why township traders
still prefer cash —
and what it costs
everyone.

South Africa has world-class fintech infrastructure. Mobile money, instant payments, micro-lending platforms — the tools exist. Yet in Thembisa, cash dominates commerce at every level, from spaza shops to formal traders. This is not a preference. It is a compounding failure of awareness, affordability and trust.

Walk through Thembisa's commercial corridors — the spaza shops, the street traders, the hair salons and hardware stores — and almost every transaction is in cash. Not because digital payment options do not exist. Not because mobile phones are absent. But because a set of compounding, deeply human barriers has made digital finance inaccessible, unaffordable and — critically — feared by the very traders and consumers who need it most.

Konecta's ground-level engagement during the Thembisa digital ecosystem diagnostic went beyond the data. Our teams spoke directly with traders, spaza shop owners and community members — and what emerged was more nuanced than a simple infrastructure or affordability gap. The cost of POS machines was prohibitive for traders operating on thin margins. Cash dominance was extreme across every category of commerce. But two findings stood apart from the purely economic: an emotional attachment to holding physical cash, and a deep, specific fear of cybercrime that was actively preventing digital adoption even among traders who could afford to go digital.

"Cash in hand feels safe. Digital money feels exposed. Until township traders trust that their money is safer in a digital wallet than in their pocket — and that digital systems won't be used to rob them — no fintech product will achieve meaningful adoption."

A compounding problem, not a single barrier

What made the Thembisa financial services findings particularly significant was not any single barrier — it was the way the barriers compounded each other, creating a loop that is very difficult to exit from any single intervention point. Understanding the loop is the prerequisite for designing an intervention that actually breaks it.

01
The High Cost of POS — Digital Payments Are Economically Punishing for Small Traders
The upfront cost of a point-of-sale device — and the ongoing transaction fees associated with card payments — represents a meaningful financial burden for spaza shops and informal traders whose margins are already thin. Unlike large retailers who can absorb card fees across high transaction volumes, a small township trader processing low-value sales finds that digital payment infrastructure costs more than it saves. The rational response is to stay in cash. The digital payments industry has not yet built a pricing model that works for this market.
↓
02
Emotional Attachment to Physical Cash — This Is Not Irrational
One of the most important findings from Konecta's community engagement in Thembisa was this: for many traders and residents, the preference for cash is not merely economic. It is emotional. Physical cash is tangible, controllable and immediate — you can see it, count it, hold it, and know with certainty that it is yours. Digital money feels abstract, distant and vulnerable. This psychological dimension of cash preference is rarely addressed in financial inclusion strategies — but it is a real adoption barrier that awareness campaigns and product features alone cannot overcome.
↓
03
Fear of Cybercrime — Specific, Informed and Growing
South Africa has one of the highest rates of cybercrime in Africa, and township communities are acutely aware of it. Scams, SIM swaps, phishing attacks and digital fraud are not abstract threats — they are discussed in communities, experienced by neighbours and amplified through social media. The fear of losing money to cybercrime is a primary reason township residents and traders cite for avoiding digital financial services. This is not ignorance — it is an informed risk assessment in a context where fraud victims have limited recourse and cannot afford to lose what little they have.
↓
04
Low Awareness of Mobile Money — Products Are Effectively Invisible
Mobile money products, digital wallets and instant payment tools have negligible awareness among Thembisa's trader community. Products that are unknown cannot be adopted — regardless of how well designed they are. The marketing and distribution strategies of most fintech operators are built for smartphone-native, formally employed users. They do not reach township traders through the channels those traders actually use: community radio, WhatsApp groups, stokvels, taxi ranks and peer conversation.
↓
05
Cash Dominance Locks In — And Grows More Entrenched Over Time
Each of the above barriers reinforces the default: cash. And cash has its own compounding logic. When a trader's customers all pay in cash, there is no commercial pressure to adopt digital payments. When a trader's suppliers accept cash, there is no supply chain incentive. When a neighbour's phone was cleaned out by a SIM swap scam, the evidence against going digital feels overwhelming. The ecosystem locks itself in — and with every year that passes without trusted, affordable, community-embedded digital financial services, the lock deepens.

What the cost of cash actually looks like

The cash preference is not without cost — to traders, to consumers and to the broader township economy. These costs are mostly invisible because they are normalised, but they are real and quantifiable.

Cost Category
Impact on Traders
What Digital Finance Resolves
Cash handling risk
Theft, robbery and loss during transit to banks or suppliers. Township traders carrying daily takings are a consistent target.
Digital payments eliminate physical cash exposure entirely. Value moves electronically, safely.
Banking access cost
Time and transport cost of reaching bank branches to deposit cash — often significant given branch scarcity in township areas.
Mobile banking and digital wallets eliminate the branch visit requirement entirely.
Credit invisibility
Cash-only traders have no digital transaction history, making them invisible to credit assessment systems. They cannot access working capital or growth finance.
Digital transaction records create a credit profile that unlocks micro-lending, invoice finance and growth capital.
Market exclusion
Businesses that cannot accept digital payments are excluded from e-commerce, delivery platforms and corporate supply chains that require digital invoicing.
Digital payment capability opens B2B and B2C digital markets that are currently completely inaccessible.
Growth ceiling
Cash businesses cannot easily scale — cash management complexity grows with volume, and funding access is blocked by the absence of financial records.
Digitised finance is the foundation for scale: funding access, supply chain integration and market expansion all depend on it.

The intervention model that breaks the loop

The Thembisa Digital Development Strategy's financial services pillar does not approach the problem as a single-point intervention — because the evidence makes clear that no single intervention will break a compounding loop. The strategy instead proposes a layered approach that addresses each barrier simultaneously, in a sequenced and mutually reinforcing way.

Digital Financial Inclusion Strategy · Thembisa Implementation Framework
Breaking the cash loop requires simultaneous intervention across all four barriers
Awareness without affordability produces informed non-adoption. Affordability without literacy produces confused rejection. Literacy without trust produces cautious avoidance. The model must address all four layers — and sequence them deliberately to build momentum toward adoption.
Layer 01 · Awareness
Community-Based Financial Literacy Campaigns
Peer-led, vernacular-language awareness programmes deployed through existing community structures — stokvels, churches, trader associations, taxi ranks. Not corporate marketing. Community conversation about specific products and their specific benefits for traders and residents.
Layer 02 · Affordability
Township-Appropriate Pricing Partnerships
Negotiated partnerships with fintech operators and mobile money providers to develop transaction fee structures appropriate for high-volume, low-margin township traders. The current pricing model is built for a different customer. It needs to be rebuilt for this one.
Layer 03 · Trust
Demonstration & Peer Proof
Township traders trust other township traders. The most effective trust-building mechanism is not institutional endorsement — it is the visible success of a peer. A structured early-adopter programme that creates visible local champions of digital finance generates the social proof that advertising cannot.
Layer 04 · Literacy
Hands-On Digital Finance Training
Embedded within the Technopreneurship Programme — practical, applied training in digital payment tools, mobile banking, FICA requirements and digital record-keeping. Not classroom theory. Traders learning with the actual tools they will use, in environments they trust, with support from peers and mentors.

The fintech sector's untapped township market

There is another dimension to this story that belongs in the conversation with private sector partners and investors: the scale of the untapped market that the cash dominance problem represents.

South Africa's township economies collectively represent hundreds of billions of rands in annual commerce. The overwhelming majority of this value moves in cash — outside formal financial systems, invisible to credit assessors, untrackable by the tax system, and inaccessible to the platforms that could transform it into digital economic activity. Every rand of township commerce that moves to digital payments is a rand that enters the formal financial ecosystem, becomes credit-assessable, generates a transaction fee for an operator, and creates data that can be used to build better products for underserved communities.

The fintech operators who build genuine trust and distribution in South Africa's township economies — who price appropriately, partner with community structures, and invest in literacy — will access a market that is currently functionally closed to them. This is not philanthropy. It is the most significant untapped market in South African financial services.

Konecta's role in the Digital Thembisa Strategy positions us to broker exactly these partnerships — between fintech operators and township trader communities, between micro-lenders and aspiring township entrepreneurs, between mobile money platforms and the stokvels and trader associations that are the real social infrastructure of township commerce.
