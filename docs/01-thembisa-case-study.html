The Challenge

A thriving township,
digitally underserved

Thembisa is one of Gauteng's most populous townships — a hive of commerce, culture and entrepreneurial energy. Yet beneath this vitality lay a stark digital divide: limited broadband access, low digital literacy, cash-dominated commerce, and fragmented government policy failing to catalyse private sector investment.

The City of Ekurhuleni recognised that digital transformation could not be left to chance. A structured, evidence-based strategy was needed — one that would integrate infrastructure investment, skills development, financial inclusion and regulatory coherence into a single, implementable blueprint. The World Bank Group appointed Konecta Group as lead project manager to design, coordinate and deliver that strategy alongside the City of Ekurhuleni, National Treasury and the Cities Support Programme.

Konecta's Role

Lead project manager
across the full ecosystem

Appointed by the World Bank Group, Konecta held end-to-end project management responsibility — from scoping and stakeholder mobilisation through to strategy validation and publication. This required navigating the competing timelines, mandates and institutional cultures of a multilateral development organisation, municipal government and an active community of residents and entrepreneurs.

The strategy was structured around the World Bank's DE4A (Digital Economy for Africa) framework, contextualised to the specific realities of a South African township — a crucial adaptation that the World Bank's own specialists identified as the key to local relevance and implementability.

P1
Digital Infrastructure
Scoped investment gaps, facilitated PPP dialogue, and identified community-led models to protect infrastructure against vandalism — a uniquely township-level challenge rarely addressed in national frameworks.
P2
Digital Skills & Literacy
Mapped skills deficits across residents, SMMEs and city officials. Designed targeted interventions including women-focused digital skills programmes and curricula integration for institutions of learning.
P3
Digital Platforms
Identified the failure points of existing government platforms — poor UX, distrust, inaccessibility — and recommended a consolidated, user-centric e-commerce and e-governance platform for Thembisa businesses and residents.
P4
Digital Financial Services
Diagnosed the cash-dominance problem, financial literacy gaps and high banking costs squeezing township traders. Designed a strategy for mobile banking expansion, micro-lending access and SMME financial literacy programming.
P5
Digital Entrepreneurship & Innovation
Elevated the eKasi Labs ecosystem, removed funding access barriers, and built a strategy for sector-wide incubation, accelerator programmes and startup investment facilitation through PPPs.
IN
Institutional Pillar: Regulatory Environment
Identified fragmentation in policy implementation as the root inhibitor of digital growth. Designed a regulatory harmonisation framework to align existing policies with investment incentives, data protection and cybersecurity readiness.
Key Insights

What we learned that
others need to know

The Thembisa project surfaced a set of structural insights that apply to township digital economy programmes across South Africa and the broader continent. These are not theoretical — they emerged from direct diagnostic work, community engagement and institutional negotiation.

01
National frameworks need township-level translation
The World Bank's DE4A framework is designed at national scale and loses critical granularity at township level. The most valuable intervention is contextualising it: identifying the specific barriers, actors and levers in that particular community — not applying the framework wholesale.
02
Digital inclusion is a people problem, not a technology problem
Infrastructure is necessary but not sufficient. The persistence of cash transactions, distrust of digital platforms and avoidance of formal financial systems stem from literacy gaps, cultural attitudes and poor UX design — not the absence of network coverage. Strategy must address both supply and demand simultaneously.
03
Vandalism is a strategic risk, not just an operational nuisance
The destruction of fibre and digital infrastructure is the single greatest threat to broadband expansion in townships. Effective strategy requires community-led ownership models — residents protecting infrastructure they benefit from — not just improved physical security.
04
Entrepreneurship is the pillar that creates sustainable jobs
In a township context, digital entrepreneurship — not e-governance — is the most direct lever for economic impact. Strategies that centre incubation, accelerator access, funding pathways and market connectivity for township entrepreneurs generate both economic growth and community ownership of digital transformation.
05
Policy fragmentation is the hidden inhibitor
Ekurhuleni's regulatory landscape is substantive — but its policies operate in silos, rarely reinforcing one another. The strategic intervention is not new policy, but harmonised implementation: aligning existing frameworks so they collectively catalyse private investment and accelerate digital development.
06
Change management determines whether e-services survive
In townships where government platforms have repeatedly failed to deliver, adoption depends on demonstrated value — not awareness campaigns. Sustainable digital services require parallel investment in change management, ongoing community outreach, and continuous UX improvement informed by actual resident feedback.

Project Lessons · For Other Cities & Funders

What this project
teaches future programmes

Stakeholder co-creation is non-negotiable
Active participation from the full ecosystem — residents, entrepreneurs, city officials, national treasury and multilateral partners — was the defining factor in producing a strategy that communities will own and implement. Top-down digital strategies without co-creation consistently fail at the adoption stage.
A nodal implementation agency must be established early
Strategy execution requires a single, mandated entity accountable for implementation — an apex agency with the authority and resources to convene stakeholders, hold private sector partners to account, and report to Council. Without this, strategies remain on paper.
Contingency planning must be built into project design
Securing participation from relevant officials, approvals and institutional sign-off proved to be a persistent friction point. Future programmes — and their funders — must plan explicitly for institutional delays and build contingency time into every milestone.
Digital technology must solve real problems, visibly
Communities do not adopt digital tools because they are told to — they adopt tools that demonstrably solve problems they already experience. The message to communities must lead with the problem being solved, not with the technology or the project.
Data-backed strategy enables accountable implementation
The baseline study underpinning this strategy — the Thembisa Township Digital Economy Baseline Report 2024 — was essential. Strategies built on evidence are implementable and measurable. Funders should require baseline diagnostics before strategy funding.
