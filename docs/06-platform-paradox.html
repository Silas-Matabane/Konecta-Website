The Platform
Paradox.
Tools that exist,
communities that can't use them.

The City of Ekurhuleni has digital platforms. Township e-commerce platforms like Kasi D and Swypa are live. The tools exist. But adoption is critically low — because no one was taught how to use them, no community member was asked how to design them, and every single one of them is in English in a Zulu, Sotho and Tsonga-speaking community.

The City of Ekurhuleni has digital platforms. This is an important and often overlooked starting point. The municipality has invested in e-government infrastructure — portals, online services, digital interfaces designed to bring city services closer to residents without requiring a physical office visit. The platforms exist. They are live. They are, in theory, available to every Thembisa resident with a smartphone.

And almost no one uses them.

In our diagnostic work across Thembisa, platform adoption was the most consistently broken link in the digital ecosystem — not because platforms were absent, but because the two barriers that determine adoption had never been adequately addressed: platform responsiveness and digital skills. Residents who attempted to use city platforms encountered slow, unresponsive interfaces that did not resolve their queries — and lacked the digital literacy to navigate the complexity when things went wrong. The result was abandonment. And once abandoned, digital services are very difficult to reintroduce to communities that have already decided they do not work.

"The City built the platforms. Residents tried them, got no response, and stopped. That moment of non-responsiveness — when a resident submitted a query and heard nothing back — is where digital government trust goes to die."

Three failures, one root cause

Failure 01
Platforms That Exist But Don't Respond
The City of Ekurhuleni has invested in digital platforms and e-government infrastructure. The problem is not their absence — it is their behaviour when used. Residents who attempt to engage with city platforms consistently encounter slow load times, queries that disappear into a void, and a critical absence of responsiveness: no confirmation, no follow-up, no resolution. The experience of submitting a request and hearing nothing destroys trust faster than any failure of awareness ever could. It tells residents, unambiguously, that the city is not listening.
Result: Investment in platforms produces no improvement in service experience
Failure 02
Skills Gap Means Platforms Are Inaccessible Even When Working
Even where platforms function as designed, the digital literacy required to navigate them has not been built in the communities they serve. Completing an online form, attaching a document, interpreting an error message, creating an account — these actions that seem trivial to a digitally fluent user represent significant barriers for residents whose digital skills were never formally developed. The platform and the user are operating at different levels of readiness, and the platform was built for the wrong level.
Result: Platform reach is structurally limited to the already digitally capable
Failure 03
No E-Commerce Presence for Township Businesses
Thembisa's formal and informal traders sell locally, to foot traffic, in the physical geography of the township. The digital marketplace — where South African consumers increasingly discover, evaluate and purchase goods and services — is simply absent for them. Not because demand does not exist, but because the pathway into e-commerce requires digital literacy, digital payments, logistics coordination and platform navigation skills that have not yet been developed in the township business community.
Result: Township traders locked out of the fastest-growing retail channel

The two root causes — and why they must be fixed together

Beneath the platform adoption failure in Thembisa are two root causes that are distinct but deeply connected. Fixing one without the other produces no improvement in adoption. Both must be addressed simultaneously.

The first is platform responsiveness. A government platform that accepts a query but provides no response is worse than no platform at all — because it creates a specific, personal experience of failure that generates lasting distrust. The City of Ekurhuleni's platforms need not only to be live, but to be monitored, staffed and capable of providing residents with actual resolution — not just submission. Every unanswered query is an anti-adoption event that affects not only the resident who submitted it, but everyone they tell.

The second is digital skills. The platform responsiveness problem affects residents who try to use platforms and get no response. The skills problem affects residents who never try at all — because they do not have the confidence, literacy or support to navigate a digital interface without assistance. These are two different populations requiring two different interventions, and the strategy must address both.

The e-commerce market already exists — the businesses just aren't in it

Here is what makes the township e-commerce gap so urgent: the market has already been proven. Platforms like Kasi D and Swypa — township-focused delivery services operating in communities like Thembisa — demonstrate conclusively that township consumers want to order online, pay digitally and receive goods at home. Demand is not the problem. It never was.

The problem is that the number of township businesses available on these platforms remains critically low. The supply side of the equation is underdeveloped — not because township traders cannot serve this demand, but because they have not been equipped, supported or enabled to participate in it digitally. The result is a market with proven consumer demand and inadequate local supply — a gap that is currently being filled by businesses from outside the township, extracting value from a community that generated the demand in the first place.

Proven
Township e-commerce market — Kasi D, Swypa and similar platforms confirm active consumer demand for digital ordering and delivery in township areas
Low
Township business participation on these platforms — the supply side is critically underdeveloped relative to demonstrated consumer demand
Now
The opportunity to get township businesses online and participating in a market that already exists — before external operators capture it entirely

This reframes the e-commerce challenge entirely. It is not about convincing township communities that e-commerce is relevant — they are already using it as consumers. It is about ensuring that the economic value generated by township consumer demand flows back into township businesses, not out of the community. Getting more Thembisa traders onto platforms like Kasi D and Swypa — and building the digital skills, payment infrastructure and logistics capability to support them — is one of the most direct economic development interventions available.

Nobody was taught. Nobody was asked.

Two of the most striking gaps in Thembisa's platform landscape have nothing to do with technology. They are about process — specifically, the two processes that determine whether a community will ever adopt a digital platform: user education and co-design.

On user education: none of the digital platforms available to Thembisa residents — government or commercial — were accompanied by structured, community-level onboarding. No training. No demonstrations. No peer champion programmes. No ongoing support. Platforms were launched and left. The assumption was that availability equals adoption — that if a resident could theoretically access a platform, they would figure it out. In a community where digital literacy is low and digital trust is fragile, this assumption is not just wrong. It is the primary reason platforms fail.

On co-design: the communities that platforms are built to serve were not involved in building them. No focus groups with spaza shop owners about what a digital ordering system should feel like. No workshops with residents about what information they actually need from a city portal. No feedback loops between users and developers after launch. Design thinking — the practice of building products with users rather than for them — was absent. The result is predictable: platforms that solve the problems designers imagined, not the problems communities actually have.

"A community that was never taught how to use a platform, and never asked what they needed it to do, will not adopt it. That is not a failure of the community. It is a failure of the process that built the platform."

The language barrier: English is not everyone's language

There is a barrier that sits upstream of every other platform adoption challenge in Thembisa — and it is rarely discussed in digital inclusion conversations: language. Every digital platform available in Thembisa is in English. Government portals, city platforms, e-commerce interfaces, fintech apps — English, throughout.

Thembisa is a Zulu, Sotho and Tsonga-speaking community. A significant portion of residents — particularly among the older and less formally educated populations that platforms most need to reach — cannot read or write English with sufficient fluency to navigate a complex digital interface. For these residents, the literacy barrier and the language barrier compound each other completely: even a simple, well-designed platform becomes inaccessible when every label, instruction and error message is in a language they do not read comfortably.

This is not a niche problem affecting a small minority. It is a structural exclusion affecting a substantial share of the community that every platform in Thembisa claims to serve. And it is entirely solvable — but only if platform developers, government departments and funders treat multilingual design as a non-negotiable requirement rather than a future enhancement.

Platform Design Vision · Digital Thembisa Strategy
Six non-negotiables for platforms that township communities will actually adopt
Building platforms for township communities is not a translation of standard product development. It requires a fundamentally different starting point — the community's actual languages, literacy levels, cultural context and lived experience of technology. These principles are non-negotiable.
Vernacular-First Design
Every platform must be available in isiZulu, Sesotho and Xitsonga from day one — not English with localisation promised later. Language selection is the first screen. If a resident cannot read the interface in their language, every other investment in the platform is wasted.
Co-Design With Community
Community members must sit at the design table from the very beginning — not be consulted after the product is built. Scoping, wireframing, prototyping, testing and iteration must all include the people who will use the platform. Co-design is not a workshop. It is a development methodology.
Structured User Education
Every platform launch must be accompanied by a community onboarding programme — sustained, vernacular-language, embedded in existing community structures. Not a launch event. A programme that continues for as long as the platform operates.
Community Champion Networks
Peer adoption drives township adoption. Trained, trusted community champions — embedded in stokvels, churches, schools and trader associations — generate more adoption than any marketing campaign. Champions must be identified, trained, incentivised and supported as a core platform function.
Responsiveness as Infrastructure
Every query submitted must receive a response. Every error must have a resolution pathway. Every failure must escalate to a human being — in the user's language. In communities where institutional trust is fragile, responsiveness is not a service metric. It is the foundation of adoption.
Visible Feedback Loops
Communities must see their feedback change the product. Public acknowledgement of issues raised. Visible updates based on user input. Regular community engagement after launch. Platforms that ignore community feedback in communities that have already learned to distrust institutions do not survive.

Konecta's role in building what's needed

The Digital Thembisa Strategy identifies two priority platform interventions: a local e-commerce platform for township businesses, and a consolidated digital services platform for residents. Both are on the implementation roadmap. Both require platform development capability, community engagement experience and a deep understanding of the township context.

This is the intersection where Konecta's AI Factory, WiFi Experience Platform capabilities and digital strategy expertise converge. Building platforms for township communities is not a generic software development challenge — it requires the contextual knowledge that comes from working in these communities, the technical capability to build low-bandwidth, mobile-first solutions, and the stakeholder relationships to co-design with the communities being served.

For DFIs and private sector partners, the platform opportunity in Thembisa — and in the township digital economy more broadly — represents a convergence of social impact and commercial return. Platforms that genuinely serve township communities create the digital infrastructure through which financial services, e-commerce, government services and economic participation all flow. They are not end products. They are the arteries of the digital economy.

And right now, Thembisa's arteries are blocked. The strategy to unblock them is ready. The implementation work begins now.
