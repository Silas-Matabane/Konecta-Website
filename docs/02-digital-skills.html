South Africa has no shortage of entrepreneurial spirit. In Thembisa alone, a township of over 700,000 people, there is a lively mixture of formal traders, informal vendors, digital startups and creative hustlers. The appetite is real. The intention is there. But in our diagnostic work across the township's digital ecosystem — conducted as part of the World Bank-supported Digital Thembisa Strategy — one finding stood above the rest in its urgency: the digital skills gap is not a gap. It is a chasm.

And unlike the infrastructure deficit, which requires capital-intensive investment and long implementation timelines, the skills gap is one that targeted, well-designed programming can begin to close in months. That is both the opportunity and the imperative.

What the evidence actually shows

The Thembisa Township Digital Economy Baseline Report 2024 — the diagnostic study underpinning the Digital Thembisa Strategy — painted a granular picture of the skills landscape. Across both formal and informal business sectors, the findings were consistent: most businesses lack the digital skills and tools to leverage technology for growth, efficiency or market access.

This is not about whether entrepreneurs own smartphones. It is about whether they can use digital tools to manage cash flow, reach customers beyond their immediate geography, access government procurement platforms, apply for funding online, or build a product that competes in a digital marketplace. These are the capabilities that determine whether a small business remains small — or scales.

"A lack of digital skills means small businesses and entrepreneurs cannot leverage technology to innovate, build, scale and sustain their enterprises — regardless of how much infrastructure surrounds them."

The barriers are not motivational. Entrepreneurs in Thembisa — and across South Africa's townships — consistently express fundamental interest in digital skills development. The eKasi Labs cohort demonstrated the greatest appetite, actively seeking to design marketable digital products and services. The demand exists. What is missing is structured, accessible, contextually appropriate programming that meets entrepreneurs where they are.

The five faces of the skills gap

In our work across the Thembisa ecosystem, the digital skills deficit manifested in five distinct but interconnected ways. Critically, each of these applies not just to Thembisa, but to virtually every township and peri-urban commercial environment in South Africa.

Platform Blindness
Entrepreneurs are unaware of, or unable to navigate, digital platforms that could connect them to markets, procurement opportunities or financial services. The platforms exist — the ability to use them does not.
Financial Tool Avoidance
High reliance on cash is not purely a preference — it reflects a lack of confidence in digital payment tools and mobile banking. Traders who could reduce transaction risk and expand payment options remain locked out by skills barriers, not infrastructure barriers.
Funding Access Failure
Government and DFI funding exists for township entrepreneurs — but accessing it requires digital compliance: online applications, digital financial records, e-registration with CIPC and SARS. Without these skills, funding remains permanently out of reach.
E-commerce Exclusion
Selling online — whether through a marketplace, social commerce or a standalone store — requires skills in product listing, digital marketing, order fulfilment and customer communication. Without these, township entrepreneurs remain confined to foot-traffic markets.
AI & Automation Gap
As AI tools become standard business infrastructure globally, township entrepreneurs without basic digital literacy are already being left behind — not in the future, but now. The gap widens with every month that structured programming is delayed.

Why this pillar demands priority

In developing the Digital Thembisa Strategy, the five pillars of the digital economy ecosystem were assessed for interdependency and leverage. Digital skills emerged as the most cross-cutting: it is the enabler that determines whether infrastructure gets used, whether platforms get adopted, whether financial services reach the people they are designed to serve, and whether entrepreneurs can access the funding and markets that are theoretically available to them.

Put simply: you can roll out fibre to every street corner in Thembisa, but without the skills to use it productively, it will not transform the economy. Skills are the multiplier that makes every other investment in the digital ecosystem pay off.

This is why, when the City of Ekurhuleni and the World Bank Group moved from strategy to implementation planning, the Technopreneurship Programme was identified as the first priority intervention — not infrastructure, not platforms, not regulatory reform. Skills.

The private sector opportunity

The Technopreneurship Programme has secured city budget allocation — a clear signal of municipal commitment and programme sustainability. But city budgets, however well-intentioned, are constrained. To achieve the scale and pace that the skills crisis demands, private sector co-investment is essential.

For corporates and private funders, this is not charity. It is market development. Every township entrepreneur who develops digital skills becomes a potential supplier, customer, distributor or partner for the businesses operating in and around Thembisa. Skilled digital entrepreneurs build the local supply chains, digital platforms and service businesses that the broader economy depends on.

The co-funding model is flexible: corporate skills development levies (SDL) can be applied, B-BBEE skills development spend qualifies, and CSI contributions are eligible. Konecta, as the programme implementer, provides full impact reporting and beneficiary tracking aligned to funder requirements.

"The entrepreneurs incubated at eKasi Labs are showing the greatest appetite for digital innovation — actively seeking to develop skills to design marketable products and services. They need the programme to meet them."

This is not just a Thembisa story

The digital skills deficit documented in Thembisa is not unique to Thembisa. It is the defining structural barrier to digital economic participation across South Africa's townships — from Khayelitsha to Mamelodi, Umlazi to Soweto. The specific numbers differ; the pattern does not.

The Thembisa Technopreneurship Programme is designed to be the pilot that proves the model. The methodology, curriculum architecture and impact measurement framework will be replicable — applicable to any township or peri-urban community where the same barriers exist. Konecta's role as programme implementer positions us to carry that methodology forward, in partnership with municipalities, DFIs and private sector funders who share the vision.

The skills exist, latent, in every township. What is needed now is the structured opportunity to develop them. The time to act is not after the infrastructure is built, the platforms are launched and the regulations are harmonised. The time is now — because skills are what will make all of it work.
