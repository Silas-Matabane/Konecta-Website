Digital Infrastructure · PPP · Municipal ROI

The infrastructure

The collaboration isn't.

The problem is not investment — it is fragmentation. And the solution creates a revenue opportunity for the municipality.

Ask most people about the digital divide in South Africa's townships and the conversation turns immediately to infrastructure. Specifically: the absence of it. But in Thembisa — and across much of Ekurhuleni — the picture is more complex and, in some ways, more frustrating than a simple infrastructure deficit.

The infrastructure exists. The City of Ekurhuleni has made substantial investment in fibre optic networks and microwave links across the municipality. Multiple private sector operators — ISPs, mobile network providers, fibre-to-the-home players — are active in the same geography. The raw ingredients of universal connectivity are present.

Yet the communities that need connectivity most remain underserved. Last-mile gaps persist. Costs stay high. Reliability remains inconsistent. The digital divide endures — not because of a shortage of investment, but because of a shortage of collaboration.

"The city and the private sector are both investing in the same geography, in parallel, without coordination. The result is duplication in some areas and complete absence in others — neither efficient nor equitable."

Two ecosystems operating in silos

In our diagnostic work across Thembisa's digital ecosystem — conducted as part of the World Bank-supported Digital Thembisa Strategy — the infrastructure picture that emerged was one of parallel but disconnected investment tracks.

City of Ekurhuleni
Significant public infrastructure — operating in isolation
Substantial fibre optic network deployed across the municipality
Microwave links providing backhaul capacity across key nodes
Infrastructure primarily serving internal city operations and government facilities
Significant capacity sitting underutilised relative to its potential
No structured framework for opening assets to private sector use or co-investment
Private Sector Operators
Active in the area — but uncoordinated and partial
Multiple ISPs and MNOs operating independently across the township
Coverage decisions driven by commercial viability — leaving low-density or low-income areas unserved
Duplicating infrastructure in commercially attractive zones, ignoring the rest
No access to city-owned infrastructure that could reduce their deployment costs significantly
Absence of a shared services or co-location framework to enable efficient rollout

The result is predictable: infrastructure duplication in commercially attractive zones, and persistent absence in the township areas where connectivity would have the greatest economic and social impact. This is not a failure of investment intent — it is a failure of coordination architecture.

The gap that collaboration can close

City Assets
Fibre backbone, microwave nodes, rights-of-way, power infrastructure
The city holds the physical infrastructure that private operators would otherwise need to build — at significant cost — before they can reach township communities.
Private Sector
Capital, distribution capability, commercial operations, last-mile technology
Private operators bring the investment appetite, technical agility and commercial models to deliver affordable services at scale — if the barriers to entry are reduced.
The Collaboration Gap
↕
No framework exists to connect these two asset pools — leaving both underperforming and communities underserved.

The digital infrastructure strategy for Thembisa identifies this collaboration gap as the primary structural inhibitor of connectivity expansion. The solution is not more investment on either side — it is a structured framework that allows city-owned infrastructure to be accessed, utilised and co-invested in by private sector operators.

This is not a novel concept globally. Municipal open-access network models, infrastructure-sharing frameworks and public-private broadband co-investment structures have transformed connectivity in cities across Europe, Latin America and East Africa. What is novel is applying this model deliberately and systematically at the township level in South Africa — and recognising that the city's existing infrastructure is not just a cost centre, but a strategic asset with genuine revenue potential.

The municipality's infrastructure as a revenue-generating asset

This is where the conversation shifts from development imperative to financial opportunity — and where the argument for action becomes compelling for city leadership, treasury officials and development finance institutions alike.

The City of Ekurhuleni has already absorbed the capital expenditure of deploying substantial fibre and microwave infrastructure. That investment is sunk. The question now is whether it generates a return — or continues to sit as a cost on the municipality's balance sheet.

Municipal Revenue Model · Infrastructure as an Asset
How City Infrastructure Generates ROI Through Collaboration
By opening its infrastructure to structured private sector access, the City of Ekurhuleni can transform existing capital expenditure into an ongoing revenue stream — while simultaneously accelerating the private sector investment needed to close last-mile connectivity gaps.
Stream 01
Infrastructure Leasing
Private operators pay access fees to utilise city-owned fibre, conduit, microwave towers and rights-of-way — reducing their deployment costs while generating recurring revenue for the municipality.
Stream 02
Co-Investment PPPs
The city contributes existing infrastructure as equity into joint ventures with private operators, enabling last-mile rollout without additional municipal capital expenditure and sharing in the commercial upside.
Stream 03
Anchor Tenancy
The city commits to procuring connectivity services from operators who use its infrastructure — providing the demand certainty that unlocks private investment in township coverage areas that would otherwise be commercially marginal.

What enabling this requires

The infrastructure collaboration opportunity is identified and evidenced. The value proposition for all parties — municipality, private operators and communities — is clear. What is required now is the enabling architecture to make it operational. Our strategy work identified four interdependent requirements.

A Nodal Infrastructure Agency
A single, mandated city entity with the authority to manage municipal digital infrastructure as a commercial asset — negotiating access agreements, structuring PPPs and reporting on infrastructure utilisation and revenue performance. Without this, coordination remains aspirational.
An Open Access Policy Framework
Clear, transparent rules governing how private operators can access city infrastructure, at what cost, under what conditions, and with what service obligations to underserved communities. Policy certainty is the precondition for private sector investment.
A Digital Infrastructure Audit
A comprehensive mapping of all city-owned digital assets — fibre routes, microwave nodes, conduit, tower sites, power infrastructure — to establish the full inventory available for structured private sector access and co-investment.
Community Protection Mechanisms
Vandalism and theft of digital infrastructure remains the most acute operational threat to any connectivity investment in townships. Community-led ownership models — where residents are stakeholders in the infrastructure they benefit from — are the most effective deterrent, and must be built into any PPP structure.

Why DFIs and development partners are central to this

The infrastructure collaboration model described above requires an upfront investment in framework development, policy design and institutional capacity that lies beyond the typical municipal budget cycle. This is precisely the kind of intervention that development finance institutions are positioned to fund — and where their investment catalyses a multiple of private sector capital.

A DFI-funded infrastructure framework for Ekurhuleni does not just serve Thembisa. It creates the institutional architecture and the proof of concept for every municipality in South Africa that holds digital infrastructure assets and has not yet found a way to make them work for communities and for the city's own fiscal sustainability simultaneously.

"The city's infrastructure investment need not be a sunk cost. With the right collaboration framework, it becomes the foundation of an open-access digital economy — generating returns for the municipality while unlocking the private investment that closes the connectivity gap."

A replicable model for South African municipalities

Ekurhuleni is not unique in this challenge. Municipalities across South Africa — from Buffalo City to Mangaung, from Msunduzi to Sol Plaatje — have made significant investments in digital infrastructure that are not yet generating the economic or social returns they were intended to produce. In many cases, the gap is the same: no framework for private sector collaboration, no policy for open access, no institutional structure to manage the asset commercially.

The Digital Thembisa Strategy is the first systematic attempt to address this gap at township scale, with a replicable blueprint. Konecta Group, as the World Bank-appointed lead for this strategy, is positioned to carry the framework to other municipalities — in partnership with DFIs, national government and the private sector operators who stand to benefit most from a structured, collaborative infrastructure model.

The infrastructure is already there. What is needed now is the will, the framework and the right partners to make it work for everyone.
